Market

The market Mutable Tactics operates in is the software layer that controls how groups of military drones make decisions together — without relying on GPS satellites or radio links that enemies can jam or spoof. Today, a single soldier operating a drone swarm in a conflict zone cannot simply send commands to each unit because those signals are intercepted or blocked within seconds. The drones go dark. Expensive assets become worthless. The structural opportunity is to replace that broken command-and-control link with software running locally inside the swarm itself, so the group of drones continues executing its mission even when all external communication is severed. This is not a consumer technology or a convenience product. It is a survival requirement for modern armed forces. The addressable market across European NATO-aligned defense budgets for this specific capability — autonomous coordination in signal-denied environments — is estimated at $4 billion, and that figure is growing as governments urgently re-equip following the lessons of the Ukraine conflict. The market structure is an oligopoly: very few players can credibly operate here because entry requires security clearances, sovereign trust, and real-world battlefield data that cannot be manufactured in a laboratory.

Timing

The precise catalyst for this investment window is the Ukraine conflict itself. Before 2022, GPS-denied swarm coordination was a theoretical problem addressed by military research labs using simulated data. After four years of active drone warfare in Ukraine, it is a proven operational failure point — documented in real time, reported in mainstream press, and now cited by every European defense ministry as a capability gap requiring urgent resolution. The cost of acquiring the data needed to train such a system has effectively dropped to zero for Mutable Tactics, because the Ukrainian 92nd Brigade is generating that data live on the battlefield and sharing it with the company. No competitor without equivalent front-line access can replicate this training pipeline at any price. That asymmetry is time-limited: if the conflict ends or access is lost, the window narrows. The urgency to act now, while the data flywheel is actively spinning, is the defining timing argument for this investment.

Company

Mutable Tactics owns what this fund calls the Authoring Layer — the software that writes the actual mission logic for how a drone swarm behaves, not just how individual drones fly. This is the difference between a word processor and a printing press: the word processor decides what gets written; the printing press simply reproduces it. Mutable Tactics is the word processor for autonomous swarm missions. Their competitive advantage rests on a proprietary data loop they call the Shadow Data Flywheel: real-world flight data collected from drones operating in actively jammed and GPS-spoofed environments, fed back into their AI models to make those models better at surviving exactly those conditions. Every brigade deployment generates new edge-case data. That data improves the software. Better software wins more deployments. More deployments generate more data. A well-funded competitor starting today — even Anduril, which has vastly more capital — cannot replicate this loop without equivalent front-line access, which requires sovereign trust built over years, not months. The software is hardware-agnostic, meaning it runs on existing drone fleets without requiring hardware changes. This removes the single biggest procurement barrier in defense technology: the need to replace existing equipment. Gross margins are structurally high because the product is pure software. The Devil's Advocate challenge to this analysis correctly identified one important caveat: the moat claim rests partly on unverified assumptions about data exclusivity.
